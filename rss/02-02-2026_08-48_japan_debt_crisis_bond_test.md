Japan's bond market turmoil reflects fundamental concerns about fiscal sustainability, not temporary election jitters. With debt at 230% of GDP - the highest in the developed world - the government faces a structural crisis exacerbated by demographic aging and shrinking tax base. Record bond yields and elevated term premiums (2.8% for 30-year vs 1.6% for 10-year) signal that investors are pricing in long-term risks to debt sustainability. Expansionary fiscal policies like suspending the consumption tax will worsen this already precarious position, potentially triggering a vicious cycle: higher yields increase debt servicing costs, forcing more borrowing and further eroding confidence. The 30-year bond auction serves as a referendum on these risks, with consequences including persistent higher borrowing costs, reduced policy flexibility, potential credit rating downgrades, and constraints on fiscal stimulus for decades - fundamentally altering Japan's economic trajectory and social contract.
